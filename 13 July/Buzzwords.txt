AI Buzzwords of the Week

These are the key AI terms that appeared repeatedly across this week's biggest stories and are especially relevant for professionals in banking, risk, compliance, audit, and technology.

AI Agent

Definition:
An AI agent is an AI system that can independently plan, make decisions, and execute a sequence of tasks to achieve a goal, rather than simply responding to individual prompts.

Why it matters:
AI agents are the next step beyond chatbots. They can automate complex business processes, making them valuable for enterprise productivity but also introducing new governance, access control, and operational risk considerations.

Example:
OpenAI introduced ChatGPT Work, an AI agent capable of creating reports, searching enterprise systems, writing code, and completing multi-step workflows.

Frontier Model

Definition:
A frontier model is one of the most advanced AI models available, representing the current state of the art in reasoning, coding, language understanding, and multimodal capabilities.

Why it matters:
Because of their powerful capabilities, frontier models are increasingly subject to government oversight, safety evaluations, and stricter governance before deployment.

Example:
OpenAI's GPT-5.6 and Anthropic's Claude Fable 5 were highlighted as frontier models undergoing enhanced safety and regulatory reviews.

AI Governance

Definition:
AI governance refers to the policies, controls, processes, and oversight mechanisms used to ensure AI systems are developed and used responsibly, securely, and in compliance with regulations.

Why it matters:
As AI becomes embedded in critical business operations, organizations need governance frameworks to manage risk, maintain transparency, and meet regulatory expectations.

Example:
Several stories this week focused on AI safety reviews, export restrictions, enterprise governance dashboards, and responsible deployment of advanced AI models.

Mechanistic Interpretability

Definition:
Mechanistic interpretability is the field of research that studies how AI models internally process information and arrive at their decisions, rather than treating them as "black boxes."

Why it matters:
Understanding an AI model's reasoning improves explainability, model validation, trust, and risk management—particularly important in highly regulated industries such as banking and finance.

Example:
Anthropic's research on Claude's internal J-Space provided new insights into how large language models organize information before generating responses.

Multimodal AI

Definition:
Multimodal AI refers to models that can understand and generate multiple types of information—such as text, images, audio, video, and code—within a single system.

Why it matters:
Businesses increasingly need AI that works across different data formats rather than handling only text, enabling richer customer experiences and more versatile enterprise applications.

Example:
Meta's Muse Image demonstrated multimodal capabilities by combining natural language understanding with advanced image generation and editing.

Open-Weight Model

Definition:
An open-weight model is an AI model whose trained parameters (weights) are publicly available, allowing organizations to download, run, and customize it on their own infrastructure.

Why it matters:
Open-weight models provide greater flexibility, transparency, and deployment control, making them attractive for enterprises with strict data privacy, security, or regulatory requirements.

Example:
This week saw growing enterprise adoption of open-weight models such as Kimi K2, DeepSeek, and other models integrated into platforms like GitHub Copilot.
